1. Genetic Clustering Algorithms
Concept: Genetic algorithms (GAs) mimic natural selection, where a population of candidate solutions (cluster configurations) evolves over generations through selection, crossover, and mutation. In the CAE, each “solution” is a clustering of IPIP items or user responses, optimized for metrics like silhouette score or Actual Information Gain (AIG).

How It Works:

Population: Initialize multiple cluster configurations (e.g., different HDBSCAN parameters or k-means k values) based on Mamba-2 embeddings of IPIP items (e.g., “I am the life of the party” for Gregariousness).
Fitness Function: Evaluate each configuration using a combination of:
Silhouette score (cluster cohesion, Section 3.6).
AIG from pilot sessions (practical utility, Section 3.7).
Alignment with IPIP-300 facets (cosine similarity to known traits, Section 3.9).
Selection: Keep the top-performing configurations (e.g., top 20% by fitness).
Crossover: Combine features of two configurations (e.g., merge cluster centroids or parameter settings).
Mutation: Randomly tweak parameters (e.g., adjust HDBSCAN min_cluster_size) or reassign items to new clusters.
Adaptation: After each session, add new user response embeddings to the dataset, re-run the GA, and update clusters. This ensures the system evolves with new data, like a species adapting to its environment.

2. Behavioral Clustering Algorithms
Concept: Behavioral algorithms, inspired by swarm intelligence (e.g., ant colony optimization, particle swarm optimization), treat data points as agents that interact and self-organize based on local rules. In the CAE, IPIP items or response embeddings act as “agents” that move toward similar items, forming clusters dynamically.

How It Works (Particle Swarm Optimization, PSO):

Particles: Each IPIP item or response embedding is a particle with a position (its Mamba-2 embedding) and velocity (direction of movement in the embedding space).
Objective: Particles move toward regions of high density (similar items) and high utility (e.g., AIG from sessions), guided by:
Local Best: The best cluster assignment for each particle based on similarity to nearby items.
Global Best: The overall best clustering configuration based on silhouette score or AIG.
Update Rules: Particles adjust their positions (reassign to clusters) based on local and global bests, with randomness to avoid local optima.
Adaptation: New user responses add particles to the swarm, updating cluster positions in real-time. Clusters merge or split based on density and utility, ensuring continuous adaptation.
Scientific Basis: PSO mimics natural behaviors like flocking, optimizing clusters through distributed, adaptive interactions. It aligns with the CAE’s active inference approach, where the system minimizes uncertainty by dynamically updating its model of psychological constructs (Section 1.4).
