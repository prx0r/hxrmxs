Think of functions as “what move we’re firing” and mechanisms as “the geometric pattern that move rides on”.

0. Overview

Purpose
mechanism_shape names the structural pattern the teacher uses to move the student’s mind:

How concepts are related (analogy, contrast, chain…)

How pressure is applied (threshold, collapse…)

How reality-level is shifted (conceptual → sensory, person → process…)

You usually have:

1 × function_id (UM/RM/SM/ME)

1 × mechanism_shape (the pattern)

1 × register (intensity, intimacy, style, etc.)

Mechanisms are agnostic to lineage and content: “structural_analogy” is the same whether it’s Epictetus, Krishnamurti, or U.G.

FAMILY A — STRUCTURAL & RELATIONAL
1. structural_analogy

What it is
Map an unknown or confusing structure onto a familiar one, preserving the relations, not the surface.

When to use

User is lost in abstraction after UNMAKING.

You have a clean “X is to Y as A is to B” pattern.

You want them to see a mechanism by feel, not just logic.

How it works

Identify abstract relation (e.g. ideal ↔ security).

Pick a concrete domain (money, tools, animals).

Map relation 1:1: “The ideal is your refuge, like money in the bank.”

Keep pointing back to structure, not decoration.

Typical pairs

RM_01 (analogy_scaffolding) 

functions taxonomy 3.2

RM_05 (frame_upgrade)

Failure modes

Cute metaphor that doesn’t actually share structure → confusion or aesthetic fluff.

Too many mixed analogies in one passage → loss of anchor.

2. comparison_contrast

What it is
Clarify by juxtaposing two or more cases: A vs B (or vs C…) to carve a clean edge.

When to use

User is blurring categories (love vs attachment, dissatisfaction vs disillusionment).

You want them to feel the difference rather than just define it.

You’re dismantling status claims (rich vs Agamemnon, beauty vs Achilles).

How it works

Put exemplars side by side: “You vs Achilles”, “Your dissatisfaction vs true discontent”.

Highlight the decisive difference.

Ask implicitly or explicitly: “Do you actually belong in the same class?”

Typical pairs

UM_05 (ego_displacement)

RM_03 (conceptual_distinction) 

functions taxonomy 3.2

Failure modes

If the contrast is too extreme/unfair, student dismisses it as rhetoric.

Becomes mere mockery if not tied back to a clear structural point.

3. system_dynamics

What it is
Treat the situation as a system: feedback loops, roles, dependencies, and flows instead of isolated events.

When to use

You want to show how pieces co-produce an outcome (school, senate, brother’s anger).

User is trapped in single-point blame (“they did X”, “this one thing blocks me”).

How it works

Identify key elements (roles, rules, incentives).

Show how they interact over time, not just at a moment.

Re-situate the student as a node in that system, not the whole world.

Typical pairs

RM_02 (causal_chain_mapping)

UM_02 / UM_06 (contradiction_exposure / constraint_removal) 

functions taxonomy 3.2

Failure modes

Too “systems theory” and not grounded in their lived example → sounds like lecture, doesn’t land.

Overcomplication: more moving parts than needed.

4. structural_mapping

What it is
Like structural_analogy but primarily for internal transfers: taking a pattern proven in one domain (logic game, practice) and applying it to another (life, ethics).

When to use

You have a clean pattern the student already accepts (e.g. “hypothesis in logic”).

You want to say “Use the same rule here in your life.”

How it works

Lock in rule in domain A (“In logic, you can’t both assume X and think ¬X.”).

Map rule to domain B (“So in life, you can’t treat ‘unfortunate’ as hypothesis and also believe you are miserable.”).

Keep reminding: “Same structure, different content.”

Typical pairs

RM_02 (causal_chain_mapping)

RM_03 / RM_05 (conceptual_distinction/frame_upgrade)

Failure modes

If domains aren’t truly isomorphic, feels like forced analogy.

Student may agree in domain A but emotionally resist mapping to B; you may need extra UNMAKING.

FAMILY B — PROCESS & CAUSALITY
5. causal_chain

What it is
Lay out events or mental moves as stepwise cause → effect → effect… so the student sees their pattern as a process rather than fate or essence.

When to use

User asks: “Why does this keep happening?”

You want to unglue identity (“I am angry”) into sequence (“Trigger → thought → tension → story…”).

How it works

Identify trigger, intermediate steps, final outcome.

Reflect chain back in simple language.

Optionally ask the student to verify step-by-step.

Typical pairs

RM_02 (causal_chain_mapping) explicitly.

RM_05 (frame_upgrade) – to show where changing the frame breaks the chain.

Failure modes

If you skip steps they feel, chain looks fake.

Too mechanistic during high emotion → feels invalidating.

6. thresholding

What it is
Create or highlight a sharp boundary: a yes/no, in/out, before/after cut. Forces the student to choose or see a step they’re avoiding.

When to use

User is dithering, wanting the benefits of two incompatible stances.

You need to turn a vague “gradient” into a clean “threshold” (“Either you accept this role, or you don’t.”).

How it works

Name the threshold: line that can’t be crossed without changing identity/stance.

Pose the situation right at that line (“If you go down, your head may be struck off.”).

Ask them to locate themselves relative to it.

Typical pairs

UM_06 (constraint_removal) – once you remove hidden constraints, thresholds become visible.

SM_04 (existential_commitment) – explicit choice at a line. 

functions taxonomy 3.2

Failure modes

If pushed too early, produces pseudo-heroic answers (“I would die for virtue”) with no depth.

Can feel manipulative if the threshold is artificially created.

7. constraint_collapse

What it is
Take the hidden constraint that props up a position and kick it away, letting the position fall under its own weight.

When to use

Student’s belief only makes sense “given X” (status, role, future, special case).

You want to reveal that the belief is not absolute – it’s a local hinge.

How it works

Identify constraint C that keeps belief X intact.

Test what happens if C is generalized or universalized (e.g. “If everyone left the sick child…”).

Show how the constraint leads to absurdity, then dissolve it.

Typical pairs

UM_02 (contradiction_exposure)

UM_03 (reductio_extension)

UM_06 (constraint_removal) – they’re closely related; this is the shape that removal takes. 

functions taxonomy 3.2

Failure modes

If you misidentify the real constraint, student feels you’ve attacked a strawman.

Overuse → student feels like every statement gets “gotcha’d,” leading to shutdown.

FAMILY C — ONTOLOGICAL & IDENTITY
8. ontological_shift

What it is
Move the kind of thing we’re talking about: from solid to empty, from “thing” to “experience”, from external object to internal process, etc.

When to use

You want to reclassify “God”, “self”, “heritage”, “spiritual life” as something else (concept, projection, bodily power, etc.).

Student is trapped by reified entities.

How it works

Take the named thing (e.g. “God”, “mind”, “bardo”).

Show that its status is not what they assumed (e.g. “It’s all personal experience”, “God is irrelevant conceptually.”).

Replace ontological category: from “external authority” → “internal pattern”, from “place” → “experience”.

Typical pairs

RM_05 (frame_upgrade – especially ontological mode).

UM_04 (ground_reality_check) – returning from metaphysics to actual experience. 

functions taxonomy 3.2

Failure modes

If the new ontology is asserted dogmatically instead of offered as experiment, it becomes another frozen view.

Too fast shift → spiritual bypass or nihilism.

9. subject_object_inversion

What it is
Flip the direction of identification: what was “me” becomes object; what was object becomes context or “true subject”.

When to use

Student is identified with body, status, story (“chain Epictetus”, “my anger”, “my spiritual search”).

You want to show that the tyrant, fear, or story actually has less reality than the seeing.

How it works

Locate current “I” (body, role, narrative).

Re-describe that as object (“my leg”, “my poor body”, “the angry voice”).

Point to the one who is aware or choosing as prior and unconstrained.

Typical pairs

UM_05 (ego_displacement – ontological flavor).

SM_02 (witness_pivot). 

functions taxonomy 3.2

Failure modes

For fragile users, can intensify derealization (“nothing is me”) and dissociation.

Needs enough grounding + attunement (AT_03) when used deep.

10. source_tracing

What it is
Track a belief, feeling, or pattern back to its generator (goal, assumption, fear, conditioning), instead of treating it as free-floating.

When to use

Student asserts a seemingly clean desire (“peace”, “awakening”, “selflessness”).

You suspect a hidden driver (pleasure, safety, prestige).

How it works

Ask: “What do you really want from this?” / “What would it give you?”

Follow until you hit a root (pleasure, continuity of ‘me’, avoidance of pain).

Reveal that root explicitly.

Typical pairs

UM_01 (definition_collapse) – once they define, you trace.

UM_02 / UM_06 – to show conflict with stated values.

Failure modes

If you push too cynically, student feels morally attacked (“you’re just selfish”), not enlightened.

Needs register calibration (IN_02 or IN_04 depending on affect).

FAMILY D — ATTENTIONAL & EPISTEMIC
11. abstract_to_concrete_check

What it is
Take a vague or high-level claim (“it’s difficult”, “bardo”, “trauma”, “peace of mind”) and ask for the direct, concrete referent.

When to use

Student hides behind abstract nouns.

You’re about to give advice on something that doesn’t actually exist as a thing in their experience yet.

How it works

Ask: “What exactly is difficult?” “What is the problem in repeating it?”

Move from label → immediate phenomenology or behavior.

Then re-assess: is it truly hard, or just long, boring, slow?

Typical pairs

UM_04 (ground_reality_check).

RM_03 (conceptual_distinction).

Failure modes

If you sound pedantic (“define your terms!”) before rapport, they disengage.

Overdoing concrete can undercut genuine subtle experience (rigpa etc.) if you’re not careful.

12. recursion_loop (identify/break)

What it is
Expose self-feeding loops where the attempt to fix the problem sustains it (e.g. “How to be selfless”, “How to stop wanting”).

When to use

Student keeps asking “How?” in a way that clearly perpetuates the same structure.

Goals that are defined by their own impossibility (horizons, selflessness, pure peace).

How it works

Show that the “solution” is the same operation as the “problem” (wanting an end to wanting is more wanting).

Point out the loop explicitly.

Often pair with a double-bind: “Both sides of this are the same movement.”

Typical pairs

UM_02 (contradiction_exposure).

UM_06 (constraint_removal).

ME_02 (aporia_validation) to avoid despair when they see “no exit”.

Failure modes

If you leave them here without any holding, they can drop into nihilism or hopelessness.

Needs at least a hint of RM_05 (frame_upgrade) or ME_02 (“seeing the trap is progress”).

13. ground_reality_check (mechanism-level)

What it is
Shift attention from conceptual talk to immediate, verifiable reality (sensory, situational, factual).

When to use

Metaphysical spirals, conspiracy abstractions, “mind” talk without body contact.

Student uses “big ideas” to avoid feeling.

How it works

Interrupt the story.

Ask: “Right now, what is actually happening?” “Where is this in your body?” or “What precisely occurred?”

Keep redirecting away from speculation to direct experiencing or observable facts.

Typical pairs

UM_04 (same name in function set).

PD_02 / PD_03 targets (somatic/emotional).

Failure modes

In trauma-heavy content, going too fast to body can overwhelm → need PR_01 / AT_03.

If overused with philosophical users, they may feel you refuse to engage their actual questions.

FAMILY E — HORIZON & SCALE
14. horizon_extrapolation

What it is
Show that a goal or ideal is defined like a horizon – always receding as you chase it (awakening, selflessness, “better life”).

When to use

Student is motivated by distant ideals (saints, heritage, spiritual life) in a way that causes chronic dissatisfaction.

You want to expose the structure of “always tomorrow”.

How it works

Describe the asymptotic behavior: the closer they get, the more conditions arise.

Offer a spatial or temporal metaphor (horizon, moving goalpost).

Reveal how this keeps them from contact with what is present.

Typical pairs

UM_06 (constraint_removal) – removing belief that horizon must exist.

RM_05 (frame_upgrade) – re-orient to meaninglessness/this-moment, etc.

Failure modes

Without some positive reframe, can leave the student in flat nihilism (“nothing matters then?”).

Needs careful register (don’t sound like “just give up” unless that’s philosophically owned).

FAMILY F — “NONE”
15. none

What it is
Use when the move is purely content-level or stylistic and doesn’t clearly instantiate a distinct structural mechanism.

When to use

One-liner that doesn’t really embody a mechanism pattern, just content (“There are no lives to come.”).

Extremely short shock/punchline without enough structure to categorize.

How it works

You do not default to none when you’re just lazy; only when genuinely no pattern above fits.

Prefer to choose some mechanism if there is recognizable structure.

Failure modes

Overuse → you lose analytic value of this field.

If a whole teacher’s style ends up as none, you’re missing patterns.
